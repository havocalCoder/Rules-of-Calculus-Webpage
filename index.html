Glitch One

Forgetting $\frac{dy}{dx}$ when doing implicit differentiation.

Have you ever tried to plug in a USB? There's only two possible ways it can be plugged in but it always takes 3 tries right? It never seems to work regardless of whether the orientation is right or not. In the same way, people have a tendency to put a $\frac{dy}{dx}$ where it shouldn't belong and forget to add a $\frac{dy}{dx}$ when they actually need one.

Remember that stuff happens for a reason.

Glitch Two

Applying L'Hopital's Rule when the function isn't of indeterminate form.

When you don't fail on the hard questions, you'll probably fail on the easy problems. After seeing that the first problem of the test was a limit of the form $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ you realized immediately that you had to use L'Hopital's Rule.

Good job!

But unfortunately for you, all of the other limits weren't indeterminate but you applied L'Hopital's Rule for all of them without checking. Well, too bad.

Either ask your teacher to stop trolling or just man up.

Glitch Three

Adding to the exponent when integrating $x^{-n}$

Sometimes, you just have a bad day and your mind's too busy with other activities. That's when this hits you. You're doing what's supposed to be an easy, 5 question integration quiz, and in one fateful moment of distraction, you write down $-5 + 1 = -6$.

That one moment, changed your entire life.

Glitch Four

Using the wrong units.

Word Problems! Since 99.9% of all high school students would never dare to touch the theoretical mumbo jumbo of maths such as group theory and complex analysis, word problems would hit them in the face to test their applied knowledge. To the 0.01% of you who will study a PhD in theoretical mathematics, don't feel lucky, because you've got worse coming to you. To the other 99.9%, pay attention because it matters now.

When given your fancy solids of revolution problems or your kinematics equations, you'd need calculus to differentiate or integrate for the solution. Don't forget that these values have units. It's all well and good when you have a standard V over T graph because you can memorize that acceleration is $m/s^2$ and displacement is in meters. But, when you get tricky graphs like velocity over time squared, you have to know what its differential equation or integral means, and what units should be applied.

However, it's pretty simple to know these kinds of things. For derivatives, you divide the dependent ($y$) unit by the independent ($x$) unit. For integration, you multiply the two. That's because you can treat $\frac{dy}{dx}$ as slope (rate of change), and $\int ydx$ as area under the curve. Just remember that THEY EXIST.

Tip: If your final answer is "The boat is moving at a velocity of 1.3 mm/s [north].", it's probably wrong.

Glitch Five

Forgetting to write $dx$ when doing integration

When you are just too lazy, forgetful, or just plain stupid. Without a $dx$ in the expression, you aren't integrating anything.

Side note: When integrating over a substituted variable $u$, you can't use $dx$ to integrate it.

$$ \int sin(x)dx = cos(x)$$

Glitch Six

Forgetting the negative sign when differentiating/integrating trig functions

That feeling when you remember to apply chain rule when differentiating a trig function but then forget the signage.

These things troll you too hard. You look hard at the different signs but still manage to screw up. It's like how people confuse (and still confuse) left for right. It causes things to crash and burn.

Remember: When differentiating, going from sin to cos is positive, and going from cos to sin is negative. When integrating, it's vice versa.

$$ \int \frac{1}{x}dx = ln(x)??? $$

Glitch Seven

Forgetting absolute values in formulas (Evaluating the integral of $\frac{1}{x}$ as $ln(x)$)

Just when you thought that you couldn't possibly have made this mistake, you realize that you forgot the absolute value in the derivative of $sec^{-1}(x)$.

Glitch Eight

Forgetting the negative sign when integrating by parts

Formulas. They're a pain when you memorize them wrong. The most tedious and time consuming way is to derive everything on the test. But sometimes, it's jsut a matter of mixing up one negative sign for a positive sign to mess up the entire question.

We get it. You had a bad day, and writing a test after that just messes you up. Just remember how to integrate by parts properly.

$$ \int_{-2}^1 \frac{1}{x^2}dx = -\frac{3}{2} ??? $$

Glitch Nine

Forgetting to check for continuity/discontinuity

It's like one of those warning labels on products that people never read. It's like the terms of service for every theorem in calculus ever. That footnote in small text that says "By the way, this only applies to continuous/differentiable functions". And granted, most of the questions you ever encounter in high school calculus are continuous because teachers aren't trolls (right?). But that one question that includes a discontinuity just makes everything else crash and burn.

Glitch Ten

Choosing the wrong F function when integrating by parts and committing the rest of life down that path

That feeling when you choose $e^x$ as your F function and wonder for the next 20 minutes why the expression isn't simplifying at all. Remember LIATE? The stupid acronym that somehow you managed to reverse in your mind along with everything else that's important? You will feel the pain of not etching that acronym in your heart.
